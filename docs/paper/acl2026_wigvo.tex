\begin{abstract}
Real-time speech translation with large language models (LLMs) has become feasible in controlled settings such as mobile apps and web browsers, where developers assume wideband audio and client-side echo cancellation. Recent end-to-end full-duplex models further push latency below 200\,ms but remain confined to broadband environments. However, deploying such systems over the Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN) remains challenging due to narrowband G.711 audio, unpredictable round-trip delays, and absence of client-side signal processing. We present \textbf{WIGVO} (WIGTN Voice-Only), a server-side relay system that enables bidirectional LLM-based speech translation over ordinary telephone calls without requiring app installation or carrier integration. A central contribution is addressing what we term \emph{echo-induced self-reinforcing translation loops}: synthesized speech echoing back through the PSTN gets re-ingested and repeatedly translated. WIGVO solves this through a dual-session architecture with deterministic silence injection and energy-based voice activity detection (VAD) gating. We evaluate WIGVO on 155 PSTN calls (148 instrumented, 147 completed) across three communication modes---voice-to-voice (V2V), text-to-voice (T2V), and full-agent---observing 555\,ms median caller-to-callee latency and 2{,}684\,ms median callee-to-caller latency, zero echo-induced translation loops, and USD\,0.28 per minute cost. The system is deployed at \url{https://wigvo-web-gzjzn35jyq-du.a.run.app} with a live demo and video walkthrough.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Streaming speech-to-speech translation has advanced rapidly~\citep{seamless_streaming_2023,openai_realtime_api_2024}, but existing systems assume wideband audio and client-side acoustic echo cancellation (AEC)~\citep{itu_g168_2015}. The Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN) remains the primary inbound interface for local businesses, hospitals, and government offices~\citep{itu_pstn_overview_2020}, yet it encodes audio via G.711 $\mu$-law at 8\,kHz~\citep{itu_g711_1988}, introduces 80--600\,ms delays~\citep{itu_g114_2003}, and provides no AEC. For foreign residents, people with speech anxiety, or users with hearing impairments, making such calls remains a significant barrier.

We present \textbf{WIGVO}, a server-side relay that bridges a web client and a standard PSTN phone number through two concurrent LLM-backed streaming sessions. The caller speaks (or types) via a browser; the callee answers on an ordinary phone---no app installation, carrier integration, or special hardware is required on either end. WIGVO translates bidirectionally in real time, supporting voice-to-voice (V2V), text-to-voice (T2V), and full-agent modes.

A key challenge is what we call an \textbf{echo-induced self-reinforcing translation loop}: synthesized speech echoing back through the telephone network gets re-recognized and repeatedly translated, creating a runaway feedback cycle (Section~3.1). Our solution combines three architectural mechanisms---directional separation, deterministic silence injection, and energy-based gating---described in Section~3.2.

We evaluate WIGVO on 147 completed PSTN calls and report latency, echo suppression, and cost metrics. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We formalize the \emph{echo-induced translation loop} problem in streaming speech translation over telephony.
    \item We propose a \emph{dual-session gated architecture} combining directional separation, silence injection, and energy-based gating for PSTN environments.
    \item We deploy and evaluate a working relay server across three communication modes, achieving zero echo-induced loops across 147 completed PSTN calls with 555\,ms median caller-to-callee latency at USD\,0.28 per minute.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The modular cascade architecture enables language extensibility through component substitution, and the dual-session gated design generalizes to multilingual call centers, voice inclusion services, and telephony-native LLM agents.

\section{Related Work}

Simultaneous speech translation systems such as SeamlessM4T~\citep{seamless_m4t_2023}, Seamless Streaming~\citep{seamless_streaming_2023}, and ESPnet-ST-v2~\citep{espnet_st_v2_2023} achieve real-time quality but assume wideband audio from controlled environments. End-to-end full-duplex models---Moshi~\citep{defossez2024moshi} (200\,ms latency), PersonaPlex~\citep{roy2026personaplex} (70\,ms turn-taking), and Hibiki~\citep{labiausse2025hibiki} (near-human-interpreter French$\to$English)---all require clean 24\,kHz audio and do not address G.711 codec artifacts, telephony echo, or narrowband VAD failures. In telephony AI, Google Duplex~\citep{google_duplex_2018} performs monolingual task completion over PSTN, while Vapi\footnote{\url{https://vapi.ai}} and Bland.ai\footnote{\url{https://bland.ai}} provide LLM-powered phone agents without cross-lingual translation. For accessibility, FCC Telecommunications Relay Services~\citep{fcc_trs_2024} require human intermediaries limited to specific countries, while sign.mt~\citep{sign_mt_2024} targets sign language rather than telephony. \section{System Architecture and Echo Gating}

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the high-level architecture. A browser client connects to the relay server via WebSocket, sending 16\,kHz PCM audio. The relay manages two independent Realtime LLM sessions and a Twilio telephony gateway~\citep{twilio_media_streams_2024}. An \texttt{AudioRouter} dispatches events to one of three pipelines---V2V (streaming ASR+TTS in both directions), T2V (typed text to TTS for the callee, speech-to-captions for the caller), and Full-Agent (autonomous call management)---via the Strategy pattern.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure1_architecture}
  \caption{WIGVO system architecture. Session~A (red) translates user speech and outputs G.711 directly to Twilio; Session~B (blue) receives PSTN audio through a 3-stage filter pipeline (Echo Gate $\to$ Energy Gate $\to$ Silero VAD). A dashed control signal from Session~A activates the Echo Gate in Session~B's filter pipeline, preventing feedback loops by injecting $\mu$-law silence during TTS playback.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Dual Realtime sessions.}
Session~A receives browser audio (PCM16) and produces translated G.711 $\mu$-law for the telephony gateway. Session~B receives PSTN audio (G.711 $\mu$-law, 8\,kHz) and produces translated audio or captions for the browser. In V2V mode, Session~B performs end-to-end translation with TTS output via the Realtime API; in T2V and Full-Agent modes, it performs STT only, delegating translation to a separate Chat Completion call (GPT-4o-mini, \texttt{temperature=0}) to prevent generative hallucination---the Realtime API's tendency to produce contextually plausible but untranslated additions. Each session maintains its own system prompt and sliding context window, ensuring strict directional separation.

\subsection{The Echo Loop Problem}

In a naive single-session design, TTS output played to the callee echoes back through the PSTN after 80--600\,ms, gets re-recognized, and triggers another translation cycle---generating progressively distorted paraphrases until manually interrupted. A Pearson-correlation echo detector comparing outgoing TTS with incoming audio failed in production due to $\mu$-law nonlinear quantization and variable delays. Without gating, eight of ten test calls produced echo loops; the correlation detector reduced this to three of ten but introduced frequent false positives. Echo Gate eliminated loops entirely (0 of 147 completed calls).

\subsection{Echo Gate and 3-Stage Pipeline}

PSTN audio entering Session~B passes through a 3-stage filter pipeline (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}):

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/figure2_pipeline}
  \caption{PSTN audio processing pipeline. (A)~Three-stage filter between Twilio input and Session~B. (B)~Temporal behavior: during TTS playback, the Echo Gate replaces audio with $\mu$-law silence while Stages~1--2 are suppressed; after settling, Stage~0 becomes transparent and Stages~1--2 filter PSTN noise.}
  \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Stage~0: Echo Gate.}
When Session~A begins streaming TTS, an \emph{echo window} is activated (duration estimated from byte length, capped at 1.2\,s). Incoming PSTN audio is replaced with $\mu$-law silence (0xFF) before forwarding to Session~B. A 0.3\,s dynamic cooldown (0.5\,s for T2V) accounts for PSTN jitter, followed by a dynamic post-echo settling window (TTS duration $\times$ 0.3, clamped to 0.5--1.5\,s) that suppresses AGC recovery noise while permitting high-energy breakthrough ($\geq$400 RMS) for genuine callee speech.

\paragraph{Stage~1: RMS Energy Gate.}
During echo windows (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}B), only signals $\geq$400 RMS break through as genuine speech; typical echo energy (100--400 RMS) remains suppressed. Outside echo windows, a 150~RMS threshold filters line noise. A three-level interrupt priority (callee $>$ caller $>$ AI TTS) enables natural turn-taking.

\paragraph{Stage~2: Silero VAD.}
Frames passing the energy gate are processed by Silero VAD~\citep{silero_vad_2021}. PSTN audio (8\,kHz) is upsampled to 16\,kHz via zero-order hold before inference. Asymmetric hysteresis---onset at 96\,ms (probability $\geq 0.5$, three frames), offset at 480\,ms (probability $< 0.35$, 15 frames)---reduces \texttt{speech\_stopped} latency from 15--72\,s (server VAD on PSTN) to 480\,ms.

Silence injection in Stage~0 addresses a distinct failure mode beyond comfort noise generation~\citep{rfc3389_cn_2002}: if the relay simply \emph{drops} audio during echo windows, the Realtime API's server-side VAD never observes a silence-to-speech transition and remains stuck in a ``speaking'' state indefinitely. To our knowledge, this failure mode and its mitigation have not been reported in neural streaming S2ST pipelines.

\subsection{PSTN VAD Failures and Robustness}

The Realtime API's server-side VAD works well for wideband WebRTC audio but fails on the telephone network in three ways: (1)~constant low-level noise (line hum, codec artifacts) is classified as speech, causing stuck durations of 15--72\,s; (2)~the noisy telephone floor prevents energy from dropping below the silence threshold, delaying end-of-utterance detection; (3)~audio discontinuities during echo suppression prevent clean silence detection. The three-stage pipeline addresses all three: the echo gate eliminates discontinuities, the energy gate removes sub-speech noise, and Silero VAD provides neural speech detection with hysteresis. A 15-pattern STT hallucination blocklist intercepts broadcast-style Whisper~\citep{whisper_radford_2023} artifacts; the blocklist intercepted 100 hallucinations across 148 instrumented calls.

Across 147 completed calls, the echo gate activated 1{,}046 times (7.1/call) with zero echo-induced translation loops.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluated WIGVO on 155 PSTN calls (148 fully instrumented) for Korean$\leftrightarrow$English translation over a five-day period (Feb 23--27, 2026). Of these, 147 calls completed successfully; three failed and five were in-progress at collection time.

Session~A achieves 555\,ms median latency, within the range for interactive communication. Session~B, which depends on PSTN audio quality and streaming ASR, shows 2{,}684\,ms median. STT (Whisper) dominates Session~B latency: across 525 paired turns, mean STT latency (3{,}544\,ms) accounts for the majority of end-to-end time. Figure~\ref{fig:latency} shows the end-to-end latency distributions for Session~A and Session~B. The P95 (9{,}963\,ms) is driven primarily by PSTN VAD stuck events---server-side VAD on noisy telephone audio occasionally delays \texttt{speech\_stopped} detection to 15--16\,s, independent of utterance length.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/figure3_latency_histogram}
  \caption{End-to-end latency distributions for Session~A (caller$\to$callee, $N{=}739$ turns) and Session~B (callee$\to$caller, $N{=}683$ turns) over live PSTN calls.}
  \label{fig:latency}
\end{figure*}

T2V mode shows lower Session~A latency (no caller-side ASR), while Agent mode shows higher Session~B latency due to function-calling overhead.

While Session~B's ${\sim}$2.7\,s median exceeds ITU-T G.114's 150\,ms one-way threshold for conversational speech~\citep{itu_g114_2003}, that standard applies to same-language dialogue, not cross-lingual translation where additional processing is inherent. A more appropriate baseline is professional simultaneous interpretation, where ear-voice span---the delay between hearing source speech and producing the target---ranges from 2 to 5\,s~\citep{goldman_eisler_1972,timarova_2011_evs}. Our system's Session~B latency falls at the lower bound of this range. Session~B's ${\sim}$2.7\,s median reflects the inherent challenge of narrowband ASR on PSTN audio; the P95 improved from early measurements as the evaluation expanded to 155 calls across diverse PSTN environments. Moreover, the latency is structurally asymmetric by design: Session~A (555\,ms) keeps the caller-to-callee direction within interactive thresholds, while Session~B prioritizes STT accuracy on degraded G.711 audio over speed. The bottleneck is narrowband ASR, not the relay architecture. Replacing Whisper with a streaming STT model~\citep{kyutai2025stt} could reduce Session~B below 800\,ms; the relay, echo gate, and VAD layers remain necessary regardless.

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} shows the correlation between utterance length and Session~B latency ($r=0.333$, $p < 0.001$), confirming that VAD stuck events are independent of utterance length.

\subsection{Echo and Safety}

Across the 148 instrumented calls, the echo gate activated 1{,}046 times (7.1/call), corresponding to TTS playback events. Critically, we observed zero echo-induced translation loops, whereas early prototypes without gating reliably produced such loops. The echo gate permitted 354 callee interruptions during active TTS playback (gate break), confirming that the energy-based override preserves natural turn-taking. Additionally, 17 settling breakthroughs demonstrate that the conditional VAD activation (Section~3.2) successfully detects genuine speech during the post-echo settling period. A minimum speech duration filter (480\,ms hysteresis) mitigated VAD false triggers to 277 total (1.9/call). The guardrail module blocked 100 hallucinated outputs (0.68/call), including broadcast-style phrases from Whisper on low-energy input and structural noise patterns (consonant-only sequences, repetitive tokens).

\subsection{Cost}

Across the 148 instrumented calls (220.2 minutes total), WIGVO consumed 1{,}870{,}657 tokens at a total cost of USD\,61.26, yielding USD\,0.28/min (USD\,0.41/call). These costs reflect dual-session Realtime API pricing and remain an order of magnitude cheaper than professional telephone interpretation services (USD\,1--3/min).\footnote{Based on published per-minute rates for over-the-phone interpretation (OPI) from major commercial providers.} Self-hosted STT alternatives could further reduce per-minute cost; a formal user study with target populations~\citep{brooke_sus_1996} is planned as future work.

\subsection{Translation Quality}

We evaluated translation quality by comparing WIGVO's real-time outputs against independent offline references generated by Gemini~2.5 Flash, a separate model family from the GPT-4o Realtime API used for live translation.\footnote{In T2V and Agent modes, Session~B translation uses GPT-4o-mini Chat Completion (\texttt{temperature=0}); V2V mode retains the Realtime API pipeline (Section~3).} This cross-model design avoids same-model evaluation bias. From 100 calls with bilingual transcripts, we extracted 73 caller-to-callee (en$\to$ko) and 225 callee-to-caller (ko$\to$en) segments. A noise filter removed 19 callee-side segments (8.4\%) containing non-linguistic inputs (consonant-only keyboard artifacts, repeated onomatopoeia, background TV transcriptions).

Table~\ref{tab:translation} reports BLEU~\citep{papineni_bleu_2002}, chrF2++~\citep{popovic_chrf_2015}, and COMET~\citep{rei_comet_2020} scores computed with SacreBLEU~\citep{post_sacrebleu_2018} and \texttt{wmt22-comet-da}.

The lower ko$\to$en score (0.58) reflects STT errors on narrowband telephony audio propagating through the pipeline, consistent with the ASR bottleneck identified in the latency analysis above.

\section*{Ethics Statement}

All calls were initiated by the authors and collaborators for system testing. Audio was logged for debugging and aggregate metrics only, with no personally identifiable information retained beyond temporary phone numbers. For deployment with real users, informed consent, data minimization, and compliance with privacy regulations (e.g., GDPR, PIPA) are required. The accessibility use case (T2V for speech anxiety) aims to reduce communication barriers but should be deployed with user agency and opt-in participation.
